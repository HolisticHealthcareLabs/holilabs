## Course Overview

This course provides a comparative framework for analyzing six major models of national health systems, with emphasis on how system structure shapes market dynamics, technology adoption, and business entry strategy. The core analytical question throughout: **who pays, who provides, and who decides?** Every system can be understood through those three axes. The course is explicitly structured for MBA students who will enter these markets as innovators, investors, or operators.

**Key textbooks:**
- Johnson, J. A., Stoskopf, C. H., & Shi, L. (2018). *Comparative Health Systems: A Global Perspective* (2nd ed.). Jones & Bartlett.
- Reid, T. R. (2010). *The Healing of America: A Global Quest for Better, Cheaper, and Fairer Health Care*. Penguin Books.

## Module 1: Introduction — RMNCH and the Framework for Comparison

### Learning Objectives
- Understand the Access-Quality-Cost-Equity framework as the universal lens for health system evaluation
- Define what a health system is: who pays, who provides, and the relationship between them
- Distinguish social justice vs. market justice orientations in healthcare
- Situate reproductive, maternal, newborn, and child health (RMNCH) as the entry case for global health system comparison

### Frameworks & Models

#### The Access-Quality-Cost-Equity (AQCE) Framework — Gordon (Course Framework)
Every health system can be evaluated on four dimensions: Access (who can get care, and where), Quality (clinical outcomes, safety, patient experience), Cost (to individual, to payer, to system), and Equity (fairness across income, geography, race, gender). These four dimensions are in structural tension — improving one typically degrades another. Universal access without cost controls creates fiscal crisis (NHS 1980s); pure cost containment without equity guardrails creates tiered care (US OOP model). No system perfectly optimizes all four.
**Application to Cortex:** ELENA's clinical decision framework should be evaluated against all four axes for any Brazilian health context. When pitching to operadoras de saúde, the argument is cost + quality. When pitching to SUS, the argument is access + equity.

#### Social Justice vs. Market Justice — Gordon (Course Framework)
Social justice orientation: healthcare is a public good and basic human right, distributed on the basis of need. Market justice orientation: healthcare is a commodity distributed on the basis of ability to pay. Every health system is a point on the spectrum between these poles. Beveridge systems (UK, Cuba, Spain) sit at the social justice extreme. Pure OOP systems sit at the market justice extreme. Germany, Singapore, and Brazil's operadoras are deliberate hybrids.
**Application to Cortex:** Brazil's dual system (SUS = social justice / operadoras = market justice) requires Cortex to design different value propositions for each segment. The clinical safety argument resonates across both; the cost-reduction argument primarily resonates in the market justice segment.

#### The Five Healthcare System Models — Reid/Gordon
1. **Beveridge** (UK, Spain, Scandinavia, Cuba): Government both pays and provides. Tax-funded. Healthcare as right. GP as gatekeeper. Waiting lists common. CDSS highest-value target: standardization at scale, protocol enforcement.
2. **Bismarck** (Germany, France, Japan, Belgium, Switzerland): Non-profit "sickness funds" (Krankenkassen) funded by payroll contributions (employer + employee). Universal coverage but private provision. Multiple payers, no gatekeeper. CDSS value: coordination across fragmented payers.
3. **National Health Insurance/NHI** (Canada, Taiwan, South Korea): Single government payer, private providers. Monopsony purchasing power. Waiting lists for elective care. CDSS value: population health management at government scale.
4. **Out of Pocket/OOP** (India, China rural, sub-Saharan Africa, most of developing world): No formal system. Pay or die. ~50% of world's population. CDSS value: point-of-care decision support for under-resourced settings.
5. **Multimodel** (US, Brazil, France partially): Multiple models coexist within one country. Different segments operate under different rules. Highest complexity for market entry.
**Application to Cortex:** Brazil is a canonical Multimodel — SUS (Beveridge-like), operadoras de saúde (Bismarck-like), and large OOP residual. Cortex must map its go-to-market to which segment it enters first.

### Key Readings
- Donovan, D., Rosenberg, J., & Weintraub, R. (2018). *Concept note: Reproductive, maternal, newborn, and child health.* HBS No. GHDC12-PDF-ENG.
  **Core argument:** RMNCH represents the highest-mortality, highest-preventable-death domain globally; interventions at this nexus have the greatest population health impact per dollar.
- De Savigny, D., & Adam, T. (2009). *Systems thinking for health systems strengthening.* WHO, Chapters 1–2.
  **Core argument:** Health systems are complex adaptive systems; linear intervention logic fails; strengthening requires understanding feedback loops, emergent behavior, and unintended consequences.

### Expert Interview: Dr. Mindy Christianson (OBGYN/Reproductive Endocrinology, JHU)
**Topic:** RMNCH innovation and payer coverage variation for fertility

Key analytical insights:
- **Fertility coverage is highly payer-dependent.** In the US, IVF coverage varies by state mandate and employer plan — mostly out-of-pocket. UK's NHS covers IVF up to age 40-42 (varies by NHS Trust), then cuts off. This illustrates how Beveridge systems ration through age/probability criteria rather than price.
- **AI in RMNCH is early-stage but high-impact.** Portable ultrasound (e.g., Butterfly iQ) and AI-assisted cervical cancer screening are expanding access in low-resource settings. AI cervical cancer screening now matches or exceeds human cytologists in several studies — a clean CDSS analog for Cortex.
- **Collaboration between OB/GYN and pediatrics is the care continuum.** Maternal-fetal medicine bridges the two specialties; outcomes for the newborn are determined by decisions made during pregnancy. This argues for integrated clinical decision protocols spanning specialties — exactly what a CDSS should provide.
- **Global burden:** Maternal mortality remains overwhelmingly concentrated in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. The highest-impact RMNCH interventions are not high-tech — they are skilled birth attendance, antenatal care, and emergency obstetric care.

### Case Study: RMNCH — Harvard Business School
**Question:** What is the business case for investing in reproductive, maternal, newborn, and child health in low-income settings?
**Takeaway:** RMNCH is not only a moral imperative but an economic one — healthy mothers produce productive workers; reduced child mortality reduces fertility rates over time (demographic transition); investment in RMNCH has among the highest economic multipliers of any health intervention.

### Discussion Analytical Framework — M1
The AQCE lens applied to a personal/professional RMNCH experience. Core analytical skill: connecting a lived observation to a systemic pattern, then connecting that pattern to a current policy news story. This is the fundamental MBA skill — moving from anecdote to system to policy implication.

## Module 2: The Bismarck Model — Germany

### Learning Objectives
- Understand the mechanics of social health insurance (SHI) systems
- Analyze the tradeoffs of fragmented multi-payer vs. single-payer models
- Apply Bismarck system logic to market entry strategy for medical technology

### Frameworks & Models

#### The Bismarck Solidarity-Self-Governance Model — Gordon/Sibbel
Germany's system rests on two foundational principles: **solidarity** (the healthy subsidize the sick; the rich subsidize the poor through income-based contributions) and **self-governance** (providers and insurers negotiate rates without direct government price-setting). The 14.6% payroll contribution (split 50/50 employer/employee) funds ~90% statutory health insurance (GKV) through ~100 non-profit Krankenkassen. The remaining ~10% opt into private health insurance (PKV). Key structural features:
- No gatekeeper — patients can access any specialist directly
- Strict separation between ambulatory (outpatient, budget-capped by Kassenärztliche Vereinigung) and hospital (DRG-funded) budgets — creates fragmentation
- Non-profit sickness funds compete on service, not price (premiums are regulated)
- Early drug assessment (AMNOG process) requires pharmaceutical companies to demonstrate added clinical benefit over existing standard of care
**Application to Cortex:** The German market requires demonstrating clinical superiority through the AMNOG-equivalent process. Fragmented ambulatory/hospital budgets mean a CDSS would need to justify value within each budget silo separately. No single payer to negotiate with — must navigate multiple Krankenkassen.

#### The AMNOG Early Drug Assessment Process — Germany (Regulatory Framework)
Post-2011, Germany requires mandatory benefit assessment for all new pharmaceuticals. Manufacturers must submit a dossier demonstrating added benefit (Zusatznutzen) vs. standard of care. If no added benefit demonstrated, reimbursement reverts to reference price. Direct-to-consumer pharmaceutical advertising is **prohibited** in Germany and most Bismarck systems.
**Application to Cortex:** Any Cortex market entry into Germany or similar Bismarck markets requires an evidence dossier demonstrating clinical outcome improvement. This is an HTA (Health Technology Assessment) barrier. ELENA's clinical data architecture must be designed to generate the evidence required for HTA submissions from day one.

### Key Readings
- Kim, W., Mauborgne, R., & Mathys, L. (2013). *Healthcare (A) & (B): Rehability, innovation in a Highly Regulated Industry.* HBS Nos. INS265/INS266-PDF-ENG.
  **Core argument:** Rehability's experience entering the German rehab market illustrates how the strict regulatory and reimbursement environment of Bismarck systems creates both barriers and moats — once approved, incumbents are protected; the evidence barrier prevents low-quality entrants.
- Heinrich, A. (2022). The Emergence of the Socialist Healthcare Model After the First World War. In Nullmeier & Obinger (Eds.), *International Impacts on Social Policy.* Springer, pp. 35–45.
  **Core argument:** The Bismarck model's spread post-WWI was a political response to the rise of socialist movements; Bismarck introduced health insurance in 1883 as a deliberate concession to prevent revolution — the original political economy of universal healthcare.

### Expert Interview: Prof. Rainer Sibbel (Frankfurt School of Finance and Management)
**Topic:** German Bismarck model mechanics and market entry strategy

Key analytical insights:
- **The fragmentation problem is structural, not incidental.** Ambulatory and hospital sectors have separate budget pools and negotiate separately. A physician treating a patient in outpatient cannot access the hospital budget even if hospitalization would be more efficient. This silo structure is the central inefficiency of the German system.
- **COVID accelerated telemedicine adoption.** Germany was historically resistant to telemedicine (in-person consultation cultural norm). COVID forced rapid adoption; the regulatory infrastructure followed. Pattern for any German digital health entry: wait for a disruption to normalize the behavior, then move quickly.
- **Capacity planning is a staffing problem, not a beds problem.** Germany has high bed density (among the highest in Europe) but faces physician and nurse shortages. The constraint is human capital, not infrastructure. CDSS value proposition: reduce cognitive burden per clinician, not reduce beds.
- **Direct-to-consumer advertising prohibition.** Unlike the US, pharmaceutical and device advertising cannot target patients directly. Marketing must go through physicians and Krankenkassen. Channel strategy must be HCP-centric.
- **The solidarity principle is politically durable.** Germany has repeatedly considered allowing sickness funds to compete more aggressively (price competition) but always retreated because solidarity is constitutionally embedded. Market liberalization has limits.

### Case Study: Rehability — Kim, Mauborgne, Mathys (HBS)
**Question:** How does a medical technology company navigate the German regulatory and reimbursement environment to achieve market entry?
**Takeaway:** In Bismarck markets, clinical evidence generation is not optional — it is the product. Rehability's key insight: the HTA barrier is a moat. Companies that invest in evidence generation early earn durable reimbursement protection that low-evidence competitors cannot replicate.

### Discussion Analytical Framework — M2
The Rehability case is a market entry template. Embedded thinking tool: (1) identify the regulatory pathway, (2) map the evidence requirements, (3) determine the value argument for each payer segment, (4) design the GTM accordingly. Apply this sequence to any Bismarck market entry.

## Module 3: The Beveridge Model — United Kingdom / NHS

### Learning Objectives
- Understand the historical and political origins of Beveridge-model systems
- Analyze the NHS reform cycle (competition → collaboration → integration)
- Understand NICE's role as a reimbursement gatekeeping institution
- Apply NHS reform lessons to Cortex's regulatory and technology strategy

### Frameworks & Models

#### The Beveridge Model — Sir William Beveridge (1942)
Government both finances and provides healthcare. Tax-funded. Universal coverage by citizenship/residency, not employment or contribution. GPs function as gatekeepers to specialist services. Hospitals nationalized. Core principle: healthcare as a basic human right, accessible regardless of means, age, sex, or occupation. The 1942 Beveridge Report (*Social Insurance and Allied Services*) named five "giants" to be slain: disease, ignorance, squalor, idleness, and want.
**Application to Cortex:** Brazil's SUS is a Beveridge-type system. The NHS reform arc — centralized command-and-control (1948) → internal market (1991) → collaboration (1998) → clinical commissioning (2012) → integrated care systems (2018) — is a direct preview of the path SUS may travel. Understanding what worked and failed in each NHS phase is directly applicable to how Cortex should time its SUS engagement.

#### NICE — National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (UK Institutional Framework)
NICE conducts health technology assessment (HTA) for the NHS. Drugs and devices must demonstrate clinical and cost-effectiveness to receive NHS reimbursement. Key metric: cost per QALY (Quality-Adjusted Life Year). Threshold: approximately £20,000–30,000 per QALY for standard approval, up to £50,000 for end-of-life. NICE's decisions are legally binding for NHS England. This creates a single reimbursement gateway with full transparency.
**Application to Cortex:** Any UK market entry requires a NICE submission. The "NICE or Nasty?" case (MS drugs) illustrates the tension: clinical evidence can be strong but cost-effectiveness can fail if the disease population is small or survival benefit is modest. Cortex's CDSS must generate QALY-comparable outcome data — prevented adverse drug events, reduced hospital readmissions — and quantify them in cost terms.

#### The NHS Reform Pendulum — Le Grand (Health Affairs, 1999, 2002)
The NHS has oscillated between competition and collaboration since 1948. Le Grand's core insight: competition improves efficiency in elective, episodic care (single-episode procedures) but fails for complex, multi-morbidity patients who require coordinated longitudinal care. Key quote from Health Secretary Hunt (2017): "The idea of lots of competing foundation trusts and payment by results works well when you have in your mind that most of the work the NHS does will be single episode elective care, but when you're dealing with complex patients... those structures prove not to be fit for purpose."
**Application to Cortex:** This is the core argument for CDSS in complex care: the market structure that improves efficiency for simple procedures actively fails complex patients. CDSS is the coordination infrastructure that compensates for coordination failure in market-oriented health systems.

### NHS Historical Arc (from case study: "Birth of the NHS")

**Pre-NHS Historical Context:**
- **1536:** Henry VIII closes monasteries → eliminates primary source of hospital care for the poor
- **1601:** Elizabeth I Poor Law establishes local parish responsibility for the sick poor
- **1722–1848:** Workhouse infirmaries, voluntary hospitals (demand-driven, not needs-driven)
- **1848:** Cholera epidemic → Public Health Act — first recognition that health is a public governance problem
- **1858:** Medical Act standardizes medical licensure
- **1911:** National Insurance Act (Lloyd George) covers workers only (not families, not women, not hospital) — Bismarck-influenced
- **1919:** Ministry of Health established
- **1939:** Labour Party commits to a free NHS; Socialist Medical Association proposes local-authority-administered service
- **1942:** Beveridge Report — names five giants, recommends comprehensive health service free of charge

**NHS Creation:**
- **1944:** White Paper *A National Health Service* published
- **1946:** NHS Act. Structure: hospitals nationalized → managed by regional boards; GPs/dentists/opticians → independent contractors; Local Authorities → health centres, clinics, ambulances
- **July 5, 1948:** NHS inception. Original structure: 150 local health authorities; Medical Officer of Health responsible for child welfare, midwifery, school health, TB sanitoria (32,600 beds). GP = gatekeeper. Community Health Centres publicly funded. NHS Trusts serve geographic regions.
- **BMA opposition:** Objected to salaried service, local authority control, threat to clinical freedom. Aneurin Bevan prevailed by permitting private practice within NHS hospitals.

**NHS Reform Timeline:**
- **1980s:** Financial insolvability recognized. Performance indicators introduced. Waiting lists become standard.
- **1991:** Internal Market (Thatcher). GP "fundholders" and NHS Trusts given budgets to buy care from providers. Purchaser/provider split. Structure: Primary Care Trusts → Strategic Health Authorities → Department of Health.
- **1997–1998:** Blair reforms. Quasi-markets abolished. Cooperation encouraged. New agencies: **NICE** and **CHIMP** (Commission for Health Improvement). Physician payments: block contracts, team bonuses, STAR Ratings.
- **Results of 1998 reforms:** Wait times decreased; GP visit rates decreased; central agency proliferation; improved resource allocation.
- **2012:** Health and Social Care Act. Abolished PCTs and SHAs. Funds transferred to **Clinical Commissioning Groups (CCGs)** — GP-led. Post-2012 critique: "distracting and damaging" top-down reorganization; absence of system leadership.
- **2019:** NHS Long Term Plan — official abandonment of competition policy. Integrated Care Systems (ICSs) across England by 2021. Mergers of CCGs; merging of NHS England with NHS Improvement.

**NHS Reform Summary Timeline:**
| Year | Structure | Philosophy |
|------|-----------|------------|
| 1948 | Nationalized hospitals, GP gatekeeper, community services | Healthcare as right, fair and equal access |
| 1991 | GP fundholders/purchasers, compete for patients, capitation | Internal market, competition |
| 1998 | Block payments, purchasers and providers still separate, performance measures | Collaboration, accountability |
| 2012 | Clinical Commissioning Groups, more top-down | GP-led commissioning |
| 2018 | Integrated Care Systems | Full integration, end of competition |

**Lessons Learned (Le Grand):**
- Politics — the pendulum swings
- Core principles can be maintained while structure and financial responsibility placement changes outcomes dramatically
- Long-term impacts cannot always be anticipated

### Key Readings
- Le Grand, J. (1999). Competition, Cooperation, or Control? *Health Affairs*, May/June, pp. 27–39.
  **Core argument:** The NHS internal market produced mixed results — efficiency gains in elective care, but failed to improve equity and created fragmentation for complex patients.
- Le Grand, J. (2002). Further Tales from the British National Health Service. *Health Affairs*, 21(3), pp. 116–128.
  **Core argument:** Blair's reforms improved wait times and GP visit rates but increased central control bureaucracy; the tension between competition and cooperation was not resolved.
- Angelmar, R., & Bieser, A. (2006). *NICE or Nasty? Access and reimbursement of multiple sclerosis medicines in the UK.* HBS No. INS483-PDF-ENG.
  **Core argument:** NICE's refusal to fund certain MS drugs despite clinical efficacy illustrates how cost-effectiveness thresholds create access restrictions; HTA gatekeeping is political economy in technical clothing.

### Case Study: NICE or Nasty? — Angelmar & Bieser
**Question:** Under what conditions does NICE refuse to fund a clinically effective drug?
**Takeaway:** Clinical efficacy is necessary but not sufficient for NHS reimbursement. The NICE cost-per-QALY threshold is a rationing mechanism that creates systematic access disparities for high-cost, rare-disease, or end-of-life treatments. The threshold's legitimacy is constantly contested.

### Discussion Analytical Framework — M3
The **HTA evaluation heuristic**: for any technology seeking Beveridge-model reimbursement, evaluate: (1) QALY gain per patient? (2) Cost per patient? (3) Cost-per-QALY below NICE threshold? (4) Political sensitivity of disease area? Apply to any CDSS seeking reimbursement in a Beveridge-type system.

## Module 4: The NHI Model — Canada

### Learning Objectives
- Distinguish NHI (single-payer, private provision) from Beveridge (single-payer, public provision)
- Analyze the five pillars of the Canada Health Act
- Understand the waiting list problem and its structural causes
- Connect fee-for-service → bundled payment → population health as payment model evolution

### Frameworks & Models

#### The Canada Health Act Five Pillars — Federal Government of Canada (1984)
Canada's NHI model rests on five criteria provinces must meet to receive federal funding:
1. **Publicly administered** — provincial plan run by non-profit public authority
2. **Comprehensive** — must cover all medically necessary hospital and physician services
3. **Universal** — 100% of eligible residents covered
4. **Portable** — coverage maintained when moving between provinces
5. **Accessible** — no user fees or extra-billing that would impede access
Federal government is the banker; provinces are the administrators; physicians are private practitioners. Single monopsony payer with maximum negotiating power — physician fee schedules set by provincial negotiations, not individual contracting.
**Application to Cortex:** The NHI model creates the most severe supply constraints (waiting lists) alongside strongest cost containment. A CDSS that reduces unnecessary referrals and procedures has direct value by managing utilization on the demand side. As Brazilian operadoras de saúde move toward value-based contracting, they act as quasi-NHI buyers — ELENA's utilization management capabilities become the core value proposition.

#### Payment Model Evolution — Park (Course Framework)
Three-stage evolution of healthcare payment models:
1. **Fee-for-service:** Pay per procedure. Incentivizes volume over value. Creates cost inflation.
2. **Bundled payment/DRGs:** Pay per episode. Hospital bears risk for efficiency within the episode. Incentivizes standardization.
3. **Population health/capitation:** Pay per capita per period. Payer bears risk across entire population. Incentivizes prevention, wellness, care coordination.
This is a **progressive risk transfer** from payer to provider. The Maryland Global Budget Revenue (GBR) model is capitation at the hospital level — hospitals receive a fixed annual budget regardless of volume, incentivizing them to keep people well and out of the hospital.
**Application to Cortex:** CDSS is most valuable in capitated/population health models where providers bear cost risk and need tools to manage utilization and outcomes. As Brazil's operadoras move toward value-based contracting, CDSS adoption will accelerate. ELENA should be designed for this payment model transition.

#### SOTA — Surgical, Obstetric, Trauma, and Anesthetic Care — Lancet Commission on Global Surgery (2015)
SOTA identified as essential health services on par with primary care. Global surgical burden: 5 billion people lack access to safe surgery; 143 million additional surgical procedures needed annually; 33 million people face catastrophic expenditure. In sub-Saharan Africa, the burden of surgically treatable conditions **eclipses** TB, malaria, and HIV combined by DALYs.
**Application to Cortex:** Surgical decision support (appropriate case selection, post-op monitoring, complication detection) is an underserved CDSS domain. In Brazil's SUS, reducing unnecessary surgical procedures and improving perioperative safety are high-value, measurable outcomes.

### Key Readings
- Partik, P., Enumah, S., Chao, T., Rosenberg, J., Kotagal, M., Riviello, R., & Weintraub, R. (2015). *Global surgery care delivery.* HBS No. GHDC07-PDF-ENG.
  **Core argument:** Surgery is essential healthcare; systematic exclusion of surgical capacity from global health investment is a policy failure; market entry in surgical care delivery requires partnering with local health systems, not bypassing them.

### Expert Interview: Dr. Adrian Park (JHU Surgeon, Department of Surgery)
**Topic:** Canada NHI model, Maryland GBR experiment, global surgery

Key analytical insights:
- **Canada system is degrading.** The five pillars are constitutionally strong but the system suffers from chronic underfunding relative to population growth and aging. Waiting lists for elective procedures have lengthened significantly. Some Canadians cross to the US for faster care — an informal two-tier system despite the one-tier principle.
- **Market forces vs. absence of market forces — both are dangerous.** Pure market systems create access inequity. Pure non-market systems create efficiency deficits and rationing by waiting. The question is not market vs. no-market but how to design hybrid mechanisms that preserve equity while maintaining efficiency incentives.
- **Maryland GBR model as capitation experiment.** Maryland hospitals receive a globally budgeted annual revenue — paid for their population, not procedures. This has reduced unnecessary admissions and improved prevention spending. Key lesson: when hospitals are incentivized for population health, they invest in preventing admissions that previously generated revenue.
- **SOTA as global health priority.** Surgery, obstetrics, trauma care, and anesthesia are systematically underfunded in global health compared to their disease burden. Most cost-effective intervention: strengthening existing health system surgical capacity, not standalone surgical NGOs.
- **Fee-for-service is the enemy of value.** The transition from fee-for-service to bundled payment to population health is not just a payment model change — it is a governance philosophy change. Fee-for-service rewards doing more; population health rewards doing better. CDSS is only strategically critical in the latter.

### Case Study: Global Surgery Care Delivery — Partik et al.
**Question:** What is the business model for delivering surgical care in resource-limited settings?
**Takeaway:** Sustainable surgical care delivery requires integration into the existing health system rather than parallel infrastructure; the partnership model (local capacity building + technology transfer) outperforms the service delivery model (foreign surgical teams) on both cost and long-term impact.

### Discussion Analytical Framework — M4
The NHI model's central tension: monopsony purchasing power reduces cost but creates supply rationing. Embedded thinking tool: evaluate any health system reform proposal against the **waiting list tradeoff** — what supply constraint does this cost reduction create? Apply to evaluate value-based contracting proposals in Brazil.

## Module 5: The Out-of-Pocket Model — India

### Learning Objectives
- Understand structural characteristics of OOP-dominant health systems
- Analyze the Becton Dickinson global health strategy as a market entry template
- Connect low-income market health system dynamics to product/service design

### Frameworks & Models

#### The Out-of-Pocket Model — Gordon/Johnson et al.
In OOP-dominant systems, there is no formal financing mechanism. Individuals pay directly for care or go without. Characteristics: informal provider markets, high catastrophic expenditure rates, strong traditional medicine complement, extreme rural/urban divide. ~50% of world population lives in OOP-dominant health systems. OOP systems are rapidly transitioning as countries develop (China's New Cooperative Medical Scheme, India's Ayushman Bharat are national OOP→insurance transitions). The OOP model is the origin state for most of the world.
**Application to Cortex:** Brazil's lower-income uninsured population lives in a de facto OOP system despite SUS. Where SUS is inaccessible (long wait times, geographic distance, understaffed facilities), out-of-pocket payments fill the gap. CDSS that reduces unnecessary procedures has a direct cash benefit for OOP patients.

#### Becton Dickinson Global Health Strategy Framework — Kramer & Mehta
BD's approach to global health market entry: (1) Design products for affordability and infrastructure constraints of the target market, not adaptations of high-income products; (2) Build local manufacturing and distribution partnerships; (3) Engage governments and NGOs as distribution channels; (4) Measure success in units sold to low-income populations, not margin per unit. A systematic reversal of the traditional premium-market-first, trickle-down-to-low-income logic.
**Application to Cortex:** The BD framework applies to Cortex's SUS pricing strategy. SUS cannot pay premium CDSS pricing. The right question: what is the minimum viable product and price point that generates volume adoption in SUS while funding continued development for the premium operadoras segment? Design for the bottom, monetize at the top.

### Key Readings
- Kramer, M. & Mehta, S. (2018). *Becton Dickinson: Global health strategy.* HBS No. 718406-PDF-ENG.
  **Core argument:** Sustainable global health market entry requires designing products and business models for low-income markets as primary targets; BD demonstrates that social impact and commercial return are compatible if the business model is designed correctly.

## Module 6: The Multimodel Approach — United States and Brazil

### Learning Objectives
- Understand how multiple health system models coexist within one country
- Analyze the US as the canonical multimodel case
- Apply multimodel analysis to Brazil's SUS + operadoras structure

### Frameworks & Models

#### The Multimodel System Analysis — Reid/Gordon
In multimodel systems, different population segments operate under fundamentally different financing and delivery rules within the same national borders. In the US: Veterans (VA) = Beveridge; Medicare/Medicaid = NHI-type; Employer-sponsored insurance = Bismarck-type; Uninsured = OOP. In Brazil: SUS = Beveridge-type (constitutionally guaranteed, tax-funded); Operadoras de saúde = Bismarck-type (employer + employee contributions, regulated by ANS); Uninsured accessing private clinics = OOP; International/elite private hospitals = premium OOP.
**Application to Cortex:** Cortex's initial GTM (operadoras de saúde) correctly targets the Bismarck-type segment where the cost containment incentive is strongest. The SUS segment is the long-term impact opportunity. A multimodel company needs distinct value propositions for each segment.

#### The T.R. Reid Five-Country Comparison Framework — Reid (2010)
Reid visited five countries (UK/Beveridge, Germany/Bismarck, Canada/NHI, Japan/Bismarck, Taiwan/NHI) as a patient seeking treatment for a damaged shoulder. Consistent findings:
- Bismarck and NHI countries consistently outperform on access + cost relative to the US
- The US is the only wealthy country without universal coverage — a political choice, not an economic necessity
- Administrative overhead in the US consumes 25–30% of healthcare spending vs. 5–12% in single-payer systems
**Application to Cortex:** The administrative overhead insight is directly relevant: operadoras de saúde spend enormous resources on claims processing, prior authorization, and fraud detection — all domains where CDSS and AI can reduce cost.

### Key Readings
- Palfreman, J. (2008). *Sick Around the World* [Video]. PBS Frontline.
  **Core argument:** Universal coverage is achievable at lower cost than the US system in every wealthy country studied; the barriers are political, not economic or technical.
- Mossialos, E., Wenzl, M., Osborn, R., & Sarnak, D. (2016). *2015 International Profiles of Health Care Systems.* Commonwealth Fund.
  **Core argument:** Cross-national comparison of 13 health systems; most rigorous empirical basis for system performance comparisons.
- Smith, P. C., Mossialos, E., Papanicolas, I., & Leatherman, S. (2008). *Performance measurement for health system improvement.* WHO.
  **Core argument:** Performance measurement frameworks for health systems; establishes methodology for comparing systems on WHO goals.

### Discussion Analytical Framework — M6
The Reid five-country infographic assignment. Embedded thinking tool: **comparative system benchmarking** — for each domain (emergency surgery, RMNCH, technology adoption), rank each country's performance and explain the structural reason. This is the core ELENA analytical capability: comparing system performance against benchmarks and attributing variation to structural causes.

## Module 7: Population Health — Singapore

### Learning Objectives
- Understand Singapore's hybrid market/social model (the 3M system)
- Analyze population health management as a system design principle
- Apply Singapore's data infrastructure approach to healthcare technology strategy

### Frameworks & Models

#### Singapore's 3M System — Lee Kuan Yew Government Design
Singapore's health system pragmatically balances market principles (individual responsibility, price transparency, competition between hospitals) with social protection (catastrophic coverage, safety net). Three pillars:
1. **Medisave** — mandatory individual medical savings accounts (payroll contribution, accumulated for healthcare expenses; can cover copayments and insurance premiums)
2. **MediShield Life** — compulsory catastrophic health insurance (covers large, unexpected medical bills above deductible)
3. **Medifund** — safety net for citizens who cannot afford care even with Medisave and MediShield (endowment fund, interest-funded)
The "no free lunch" philosophy: everyone contributes to their own health savings; government provides protection against catastrophic risk; means-tested safety net for those who truly cannot contribute.
**Application to Cortex:** The 3M system creates price transparency between hospitals — patients with Medisave accounts make explicit cost-quality tradeoffs. A CDSS in Singapore would need to be designed for a cost-conscious, quality-tracking patient population, not a passive patient assuming everything is covered. Data architecture for a price-transparent system differs fundamentally from a fee-for-service system.

#### Singapore Population Health Management via IT — Woodard et al. (HBS)
Singapore's Alexandra Health System (AHS) built a population health program exploiting the national EHR (covering all 5.5M residents) to proactively manage high-risk populations. Key elements: national EHR as population health data foundation; risk stratification of the population by chronic disease burden, social determinants, prior utilization; proactive outreach to high-risk individuals; community-based interventions; integrated primary/specialist/hospital care coordination. Singapore as innovation hub: size (5.5M), centralized data infrastructure, government commitment to health as economic productivity, absence of political fragmentation.
**Application to Cortex:** ELENA's long-term evolution is toward population health management — aggregating Clinical Ground Truth data to identify population-level risk patterns. Singapore's AHS model is the template. Key insight: **population health management is not possible without the data infrastructure**. Building the data infrastructure is the prerequisite, not the byproduct.

#### Porter's Healthcare Value Formula — Porter (2010)
**Value = Patient Outcomes / Cost of Delivering Those Outcomes**
Key implications: (1) The unit of analysis for value is the medical condition, not the episode or procedure; (2) Outcomes must be measured over the full care cycle; (3) Costs must include all costs associated with the patient's condition; (4) Maximizing value requires restructuring care delivery around integrated practice units (IPUs) focused on specific conditions.
**Application to Cortex:** This is Cortex's core value proposition expressed in Porter's language. CDSS generates value by improving patient outcomes (reduced adverse drug events, appropriate escalation, protocol adherence) per unit cost. The Safety Firewall's false-positive rate reduction and the Clinical Ground Truth data loop are both Porter value-generation mechanisms. Frame for hospital CMOs and operadoras: "We increase the numerator and decrease the denominator simultaneously."

### Expert Interview: Prof. Rainer Sibbel (Frankfurt School) — Second Interview
**Topic:** Singapore's healthcare system

Key analytical insights:
- **Singapore's pragmatism is the model's defining feature.** Unlike Beveridge (healthcare is a right) or pure market (healthcare is a commodity), Singapore explicitly says both are partially correct and designs accordingly. The 3M system is the product of deliberate philosophical eclecticism.
- **Individual savings accounts as behavioral design.** Medisave creates cost-consciousness without denying access. When patients have an explicit financial stake in their care decisions, utilization patterns change — fewer unnecessary procedures, more preventive care. This is behavioral economics applied to healthcare system design.
- **Price transparency between hospitals is real.** Singapore publishes benchmark prices for common procedures across hospitals of different classes (A, B1, B2, C). Patients can make informed cost-quality tradeoffs. This creates genuine competition on value.
- **National EHR at 5.5M population scale is achievable.** Singapore's NEHR covers the entire resident population. Lesson for Brazil: national EHR at scale requires government mandate, standardized terminology, and strong governance — none of which Brazil currently has at SUS level.
- **Demographic planning as health system driver.** Singapore plans for a target population of 7.5M and designs health infrastructure accordingly. Brazil's SUS was not designed for its current 215M population and faces severe capacity gaps.

### Case Study: Alexandra Health System — Woodard, Dula, Zheng
**Question:** How does a regional health system build a population health program that reduces cost and improves outcomes at scale?
**Takeaway:** Population health management requires three simultaneous investments: (1) data infrastructure (national EHR, risk stratification tools), (2) care model innovation (proactive outreach, community-based services, integrated care teams), and (3) financial model alignment (capitation or shared savings to fund prevention). All three are necessary; none alone is sufficient.

### Discussion Analytical Framework — M7
Singapore vs. US comparison. Embedded thinking tool: **system design counterfactual analysis** — identify the specific structural barrier that prevents the US from adopting a Singapore-style system (political fragmentation, absence of national EHR mandate, insurance lobby opposition) and identify which barrier could be partially addressed. Analytical skill for regulatory strategy: identify the binding constraint, not the full constraint set.

### Frameworks & Models

#### The Market Entry Decision Framework — Gordon (Course Synthesis)
For any healthcare innovator entering a new national market, the analytical sequence:
1. **Classify the system** (Beveridge / Bismarck / NHI / OOP / Multimodel)
2. **Map the payer** (government / sickness funds / private insurers / OOP patient / employer)
3. **Identify the reimbursement pathway** (HTA body / formulary committee / direct contracting / OOP)
4. **Analyze the AQCE performance** (what is the payer's primary pain — cost? access? quality? equity?)
5. **Design the value proposition** (match Cortex's capabilities to the payer's pain)
6. **Assess structural risk** (regulatory change, single-payer price negotiation risk, political stability)
**Application to Cortex:** Applied to Brazil: Step 1 = Multimodel; Step 2 = ANS-regulated operadoras (primary) / SUS secretariats (secondary); Step 3 = ANS reimbursement via procedure code + value-based contracting pilots; Step 4 = cost pressure primary (sinistralidade); Step 5 = sinistralidade reduction through clinical decision support; Step 6 = regulatory risk = ANS rule changes on CDSS reimbursement.

## Cross-References to Existing Knowledge Library

### Reinforces
- **Porter's Healthcare Value Formula** (M7) reinforces **Porter & Heppelmann — Data Loop Primacy** (MBA_FRAMEWORKS.md) because both argue that value accrues to whoever controls the continuous feedback loop between patient outcomes and care decisions. ELENA's Clinical Ground Truth IS Porter's value feedback mechanism.
- **The Five Healthcare System Models** (M1) reinforces **The Four Strategic Bets** (MBA_FRAMEWORKS.md): classifying Brazil as Multimodel confirms why "own the data loop, not the app" is the correct strategy — in fragmented multimodel systems, the data aggregator becomes the indispensable infrastructure layer.
- **Sibbel's Bismarck fragmentation analysis** reinforces **Aron & Singh — Partnership Risk Matrix** because the ambulatory/hospital budget silo structure in Germany is exactly the high-structural-risk, low-codifiability partnership configuration. Any Cortex partnership with a Bismarck-system payer must evaluate whether the partner controls the budget silo that Cortex's value flows through.

### Extends
- **Payment Model Evolution (Park)** extends **McAfee & Brynjolfsson — Management Revolution Framing** by specifying the payment model condition under which the management revolution becomes financially compulsory: only in capitated/population health models do providers have the financial incentive to adopt CDSS as a cost management tool. Fee-for-service providers have the opposite incentive.
- **Singapore 3M + AHS Population Health** extends **Porter & Heppelmann — Data Loop Primacy** by providing the operational blueprint for what the data loop looks like at national population scale: national EHR + risk stratification + proactive outreach + outcomes measurement is the full cycle Porter and Heppelmann describe abstractly.
- **NICE HTA Framework** extends **Shapiro & Varian — Cognitive Lock-in and Value Pricing** by identifying the reimbursement pathway through which lock-in becomes institutionally enforced: once a CDSS earns NICE approval, alternatives must clear the same HTA bar — the evidence barrier becomes the moat.

### Tensions
- **Social Justice vs. Market Justice** creates a productive tension with **Shapiro & Varian — Value Pricing** when applied to SUS pricing decisions. Shapiro & Varian say: price on value delivered. But social justice systems prohibit differential pricing — SUS cannot pay Cortex what the value is worth. This forces a dual pricing strategy: value pricing for operadoras, access pricing for SUS.
- **Canada Health Act Accessibility Pillar** creates tension with **Aron & Singh — Partnership Risk Matrix** in the context of partnerships with provincial health authorities. The accessibility pillar prohibits user fees — this forces monetization through the payer (province), not the end-user. The structural risk of a single-province partnership is high because provincial government is the only buyer.
- **NHS Reform Pendulum (Le Grand)** creates tension with **The Four Strategic Bets** specifically Bet 3 ("Sell management transformation, not software") when the political pendulum swings toward collaboration. In collaboration-phase NHS, the "management revolution" framing resonates with leadership. In competition-phase NHS, the framing must shift to efficiency and market positioning. The bet is invariant; the framing must be adaptive.

## Tier 1 Candidates

### QUALIFIES: Porter's Healthcare Value Formula — Porter (2010) / BU.881.710

**Apply when:** Framing Cortex's value proposition to any clinical or payer buyer, or evaluating whether a partnership increases or decreases Cortex's value delivery.

**Decision test:**
- Does this feature/partnership increase patient outcomes (prevented adverse events, protocol adherence, appropriate escalation)? → Adds to the numerator.
- Does this feature/partnership reduce the cost of delivering those outcomes (fewer readmissions, fewer unnecessary procedures, lower admin burden)? → Reduces the denominator.
- Does this partnership outsource a function that currently generates outcome data? → Destroys the value formula by separating Cortex from its measurement mechanism.

**Threshold:** Any initiative that neither improves outcomes NOR reduces cost is not a Cortex investment. Any partnership that cedes outcome measurement to the partner fails the data loop primacy test.

**Key metric:** Value = Outcomes / Cost. Track: adverse event rate (before/after), protocol adherence rate, hospital readmission rate, cost per patient episode.

### QUALIFIES: Healthcare Market Entry Decision Sequence — Gordon / BU.881.710

**Apply when:** GORDON or VICTOR evaluates any international expansion opportunity or new domestic segment entry.

**Decision test:**
1. System type classified? (Beveridge/Bismarck/NHI/OOP/Multimodel)
2. Payer identified and reimbursement pathway mapped?
3. System's primary performance pressure identified (cost/access/quality/equity)?
4. Value proposition matched to the pressure?
5. Structural risks assessed (political, regulatory, single-payer concentration)?

**Threshold:** All five must be answered before committing resources to market entry analysis. If any is unknown, the first task is to answer it.

**Key metric:** Time from market identification to first reimbursement decision. Bismarck/NHI markets: 18–36 months (HTA process). OOP markets: 3–6 months. Beveridge/SUS markets: politically variable.
