## SECTION 1: SUPPLY, DEMAND, AND MARKET EQUILIBRIUM

### 1.1 Market Fundamentals

**Supply and Demand Framework:** Microeconomics studies the decisions of individuals and firms to allocate resources across production, exchange, and consumption. The fundamental market mechanism operates through:

- **Demand curves** representing consumers' willingness to pay (WTP) at various price points
- **Supply curves** reflecting firms' willingness to supply at different prices
- **Market equilibrium** where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied

**Key Principle - Decision-Making on the Margin:** Firms and consumers make optimal decisions by comparing marginal benefits to marginal costs. A firm should expand production if marginal revenue exceeds marginal cost; consumers should purchase if willingness to pay exceeds price.

### 1.2 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

**Definition and Calculation:**
PED = (% Change in Quantity Demanded) / (% Change in Price)

- **Elastic demand** (|PED| > 1): Quantity demanded highly responsive to price changes. Revenue decreases when price increases.
- **Inelastic demand** (|PED| < 1): Quantity demanded relatively insensitive to price changes. Revenue increases when price increases.
- **Unit elastic** (|PED| = 1): A 1% price change causes a 1% change in quantity demanded.

**Healthcare Application - High Inelasticity:** Healthcare services, particularly acute/emergency care, exhibit highly inelastic demand because:
- Patients need immediate treatment regardless of price
- Insurance shields patients from full price signals (moral hazard)
- Limited substitutes for critical procedures
- No time for price shopping during medical emergencies

For Cortex: This inelasticity justifies premium pricing for critical decision support capabilities that reduce adverse patient outcomes.

**Cross-Price Elasticity:** Measures responsiveness of one good's quantity demanded to another good's price change. Positive CED indicates substitutes; negative indicates complements. In the Brazilian market, Cortex's CDSS may be complementary to existing EHR systems, so EHR price changes affect demand for Cortex services.

**Income Elasticity:** Measures responsiveness to income changes. Healthcare is typically income-inelastic in wealthy markets but may be more elastic in emerging markets like Brazil where affordability varies dramatically across the private/public divide.

### 1.3 Consumer and Producer Surplus

**Consumer Surplus (CS)** = Difference between WTP and actual price paid
- Geometrically: Area under demand curve above the price line
- Indicates consumer benefit from market transactions

**Producer Surplus (PS)** = Difference between actual price received and willingness to accept
- Geometrically: Area above supply curve below the price line
- Indicates firm benefit from market transactions

**Total Welfare** = CS + PS + any externalities

In perfectly competitive markets with no externalities, equilibrium maximizes total welfare. Deviations (monopoly pricing, price controls) create **deadweight loss** (DWL), the reduction in total surplus from market distortions.

### 1.4 Elasticity and Revenue Relationships

**For a firm to maximize revenue:**
- If demand is **elastic** (|PED| > 1): Lower price to increase total revenue
- If demand is **inelastic** (|PED| < 1): Raise price to increase total revenue
- If demand is **unit elastic**: Revenue is maximized; price changes don't affect total revenue

**Cortex Pricing Implication:** If Cortex's solutions address highly inelastic demand (avoiding litigation/mortality), the firm can employ premium pricing without revenue loss from reduced utilization.

## SECTION 2: PRODUCTION THEORY AND COST STRUCTURE

### 2.1 Production Function Fundamentals

**Production Function:** Q = f(L, K, ...) describes maximum output from given inputs (labor, capital, materials).

**Marginal Product (MP)** = Additional output from one more unit of input
**Average Product (AP)** = Total output / Total units of input

**Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns:** As a firm increases use of one variable input (while holding others fixed), the marginal product of that input eventually declines.

Example: Adding software engineers to Cortex's development team increases output (features/month), but at a decreasing rate as coordination costs rise.

### 2.2 Cost Structure: Fixed vs. Variable

**Economic Costs** ≠ Accounting Costs (includes opportunity costs; excludes sunk costs)

**Fixed Costs (FC):** Do not vary with output quantity
- Examples for Cortex: R&D, salaries, software licenses with annual commitments

**Variable Costs (VC):** Scale with output quantity
- Examples for Cortex: Cloud infrastructure per deployment, customer success resources, API calls to external systems

**Total Cost (TC) = FC + VC**

**Sunk Costs:** Past expenditures that cannot be recovered; should NOT influence forward-looking decisions. Example: Cortex's initial investment in NLP model training is sunk; future pricing should ignore this.

### 2.3 Short-Run vs. Long-Run Cost Analysis

**Short-run (SR):** At least one factor of production is fixed. Firm can only adjust variable inputs.

**Long-run (LR):** All factors are variable. Firm can change all inputs and scale operations.

**Implication:** SR cost curves (ATC, MC) are typically U-shaped. LR Average Cost (LRAC) reflects the lowest-cost production scale for each output level.

### 2.4 Key Cost Metrics

**Average Fixed Cost (AFC)** = FC / Q → Declines as output increases
**Average Variable Cost (AVC)** = VC / Q → U-shaped (diminishing then increasing MP)
**Average Total Cost (ATC)** = TC / Q = AFC + AVC
**Marginal Cost (MC)** = Change in TC / Change in Q

**Relationship:**
- When MC < ATC, ATC is declining
- When MC > ATC, ATC is rising
- When MC = ATC, ATC is at minimum

### 2.5 Economies and Diseconomies of Scale

**Economies of Scale:** LRAC declines as firm expands output. Sources:
- Spreading fixed R&D costs across more units
- Bulk purchasing discounts
- Specialized production and labor division
- Network effects in software platforms

For Cortex: Significant economies of scale exist. Initial customer deployment amortizes substantial fixed development costs; marginal customer addition has low incremental cost.

**Diseconomies of Scale:** LRAC rises as firm expands. Sources:
- Management coordination costs
- Incentive misalignment in large organizations
- Market saturation effects

**Minimum Efficient Scale (MES):** Output level where firm exhausts all scale economies. For software companies, MES is typically high due to large fixed costs and low marginal costs.

## SECTION 3: PERFECT COMPETITION AND MARKET EQUILIBRIUM

### 3.1 Perfect Competition Assumptions

Perfect competition exists when:
1. Many buyers and sellers (atomistic)
2. Homogeneous product
3. Free entry and exit
4. Perfect information
5. Perfect mobility of factors
6. Price takers on all sides

**Reality:** True perfect competition is rare, but the model provides a useful benchmark.

### 3.2 Competitive Firm Profit Maximization

In perfect competition, firm faces horizontal demand curve at market price P.

**Profit-maximizing condition (SR and LR):** P = MC

- If P > MC: Firm should expand output (marginal unit profitable)
- If P < MC: Firm should reduce output (marginal unit unprofitable)
- If P = MC: Optimal output level

**Short-Run Equilibrium:** Firm produces where P = MC. May earn positive, zero, or negative economic profit depending on whether P > ATC, P = ATC, or P < ATC.

**Long-Run Equilibrium:** Free entry/exit drives economic profit to zero. All firms produce where P = MC = minimum ATC.

### 3.3 Market Equilibrium and Adjustment

**Competitive Market Equilibrium:** Quantity demanded = Quantity supplied at market price.
- Geometrically: Intersection of market demand and supply curves

**Adjustment to Demand Shock (Increase):**
1. SR: Price rises above MC; firms increase output and earn positive profit
2. LR: Positive profit attracts entry; market supply increases; price falls back to original long-run level
3. Result: LR output increases; price returns to original level (perfectly competitive result)

**Policy Implication - Price Controls:**
- **Price ceiling** (maximum legal price): If binding, creates excess demand and potential shortage
- **Price floor** (minimum legal price): If binding, creates excess supply (surplus)
- Both create deadweight loss by preventing mutually beneficial transactions

## SECTION 4: MONOPOLY AND MARKET POWER

### 4.1 Sources of Monopoly Power

**Structural barriers to entry:**
- Economies of scale (natural monopoly)
- Control of essential inputs
- Patent protection
- Network externalities

**Strategic barriers to entry:**
- Predatory pricing
- Exclusive dealing
- High switching costs

### 4.2 Monopoly Profit Maximization

Unlike perfect competition, monopoly faces downward-sloping demand. Key relationships:

**Price > Marginal Revenue** (because to sell additional unit, firm must lower price on all units)

**Profit-Maximizing Rule:** MR = MC

- Find output where MR = MC
- Determine price from demand curve at that output
- Calculate profit as (P - ATC) × Q

**Deadweight Loss from Monopoly:** Monopoly restricts output below competitive level:
- Competitive: P = MC, Q_C (socially optimal)
- Monopoly: P > MC, Q_M < Q_C (socially suboptimal)
- DWL = lost consumer and producer surplus from unexploited trading opportunities

### 4.3 Marginal Revenue and Elasticity

**MR = P(1 - 1/|PED|)**

- If demand is elastic (|PED| > 1): MR > 0; price increases boost revenue
- If demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1): MR < 0; price increases reduce revenue
- At unit elasticity: MR = 0; revenue is maximized

**Implication:** Monopoly never operates in inelastic region of demand. If it did, it could raise price without losing revenue (since MR < 0), which means it's not at the profit-maximizing point.

## SECTION 5: MONOPOLISTIC COMPETITION AND OLIGOPOLY

### 5.1 Monopolistic Competition

**Characteristics:**
- Many firms (easy entry/exit)
- Differentiated products (each firm has downward-sloping demand)
- Free entry in LR

**Equilibrium:** Like monopoly, firm produces where MR = MC. Differentiation allows P > MC in SR. LR free entry drives profit to zero (P = ATC), but firms produce at less than minimum-cost scale—the "excess capacity theorem."

**Implication for Cortex:** If Cortex operates in monopolistic competition (differentiating on AI/NLP capability while competing with other CDSS), long-run profit is limited by entry of new competitors. Durable competitive advantage requires continuous innovation.

### 5.2 Oligopoly and Strategic Interdependence

**Definition:** Few firms compete; each recognizes its actions affect rivals and vice versa.

**Key Insight:** Oligopoly outcomes depend critically on firms' beliefs about rival responses. This motivates **game theory** analysis.

**Cournot Model:** Each firm chooses quantity simultaneously, taking rival quantities as given.
- Equilibrium quantity between monopoly and competition
- Price between monopoly and competitive levels

**Bertrand Model:** Firms compete on price (common in many industries).
- If products are homogeneous and no capacity constraints: Price competition drives P = MC
- If products differentiated: Firms can maintain P > MC

## SECTION 6: GAME THEORY AND STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

### 6.1 Foundational Concepts

**Normal Form Game:** Specification of:
1. Players
2. Strategy set for each player
3. Payoff function mapping strategy profiles to outcomes

**Representation:** Payoff matrix where rows = Player 1 strategies, columns = Player 2 strategies, entries = payoffs (Player 1, Player 2).

### 6.2 Dominant Strategies and Nash Equilibrium

**Dominant Strategy:** Best response regardless of opponent's choice.
- If player has dominant strategy, she should always use it
- If both players have dominant strategies, that's the likely outcome

**Nash Equilibrium:** Strategy profile where each player's strategy is a best response to others' strategies. No player has incentive to unilaterally deviate.

**Key Point:** Dominant strategy equilibrium is always a Nash equilibrium, but not all Nash equilibria involve dominant strategies.

### 6.3 Classic Game: The Prisoner's Dilemma

**Setup:** Two suspects arrested; each can confess or remain silent. Payoffs (years in prison):

| PLAYER 1 / PLAYER 2 | Confess | Silent |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Confess** | -5, -5 | -1, -10 |
| **Silent** | -10, -1 | -2, -2 |

**Analysis:**
- Player 1 dominant strategy: Confess (prefers -5 to -10 if P2 silent; prefers -5 to -2 if P2 confesses)
- Player 2 dominant strategy: Confess (by symmetry)
- **Nash Equilibrium:** Both confess, payoffs = (-5, -5)
- **Socially optimal outcome:** Both silent, payoffs = (-2, -2)

**Dilemma:** Rational individual choices lead to collectively suboptimal outcome. Each player regrets mutual confession but can't unilaterally improve without trust.

### 6.4 Nico's Problem Set Examples: Pricing with Market Power and Game Theory

#### M4 Problem Set Example: Third-Degree Price Discrimination

**Scenario:** Monopolist with two markets. After selling all output:
- Local market marginal revenue: MR_L = $20
- Internet auction marginal revenue: MR_I = $30
- Constant marginal cost: MC = (positive, same in both markets)

**Question:** To maximize profit, the firm should:

**Answer (Correct):** Have sold less output in the local market and more on the internet auction site.

**Explanation:** Marginal profit = MR - MC. Since MC is constant across markets:
- Local market marginal profit: $20 - MC
- Internet market marginal profit: $30 - MC

By reallocating sales from low-MR market (MR = $20) to high-MR market (MR = $30), the firm increases total profit. Optimal allocation occurs when MR_L = MR_I = MC. Here, MR_I > MR_L, so expand in internet market, contract in local market.

**Application to Cortex:** If Cortex serves both high-margin segments (private hospitals) and low-margin segments (public sector/SUS), it should allocate more R&D and implementation resources to high-margin segment if marginal revenues differ significantly.

#### M4 Problem Set Example: Two-Part Tariff Optimization

**Scenario:** Monopoly with identical consumers (same demand). Demand curve: P = 50 - 2Q. Per-unit price = $2. Marginal cost is positive.

**Question:** Maximum fixed fee a consumer would pay (entry fee) to purchase at P = $2?

**Answer (Correct):** $576

**Calculation:**
- At P = $2: Q = 25 - 0.5(2) = 24 units
- Consumer surplus at this price and quantity = 0.5 × (50 - 2) × 24 = 0.5 × 48 × 24 = $576
- This is the maximum fixed fee (entry fee) the consumer would pay; the firm captures all consumer surplus

**Two-Part Tariff Structure:** Set per-unit price = MC to maximize quantity/efficiency, then capture all consumer surplus via entry fee.

**Application to Cortex:** Cortex could employ a two-part tariff:
- Entry fee: Initial implementation fee covering training, setup, system integration ($50K - $200K depending on hospital size)
- Per-unit price: Monthly subscription or per-patient-encounter fee set near marginal cost

This structure maximizes total profit by extracting consumer surplus while maintaining volume efficiency.

#### M5 Problem Set Example: Dominant Strategies and Externalities (Pollution Game)

**Scenario:** Two firms (Loch Inc. and Nessie Corp.) next to a lake. Each can pollute or not. Costs (payoffs are negative = costs):

| LOCH / NESSIE | Pollute | Don't Pollute |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Pollute** | -$2,000, -$2,000 | -$1,000, -$2,500 |
| **Don't Pollute** | -$2,500, -$1,000 | -$1,500, -$1,500 |

Filter cost: $1,500 per firm per period
Pollution damage from one firm polluting: $1,000 per firm per period
Pollution damage from both polluting: $2,000 per firm per period (cumulative)

**Analysis:**
- If Nessie pollutes: Loch's payoffs are -$1,000 (pollute) vs. -$2,500 (don't pollute). Loch prefers pollute.
- If Nessie doesn't pollute: Loch's payoffs are -$1,000 (pollute) vs. -$1,500 (don't pollute). Loch prefers pollute.
- **Loch's dominant strategy: Pollute**
- **By symmetry, Nessie's dominant strategy: Pollute**
- **Nash Equilibrium:** Both pollute, payoffs = (-$2,000, -$2,000)
- **Socially optimal:** Both don't pollute, payoffs = (-$1,500, -$1,500)

**Economic Insight:** Negative externalities create prisoner's dilemma. Each firm benefits by polluting while others don't, but mutual pollution is worse than mutual non-pollution. This motivates regulatory intervention.

**Healthcare Application - Drug Pricing:** Pharmaceutical firms face similar dilemma. Each firm wants high prices, but if all raise prices simultaneously, negative externalities (reduced access, government pressure) harm all.

#### M5 Problem Set Example: Boeing vs. Airbus Entry Game

**Scenario:** Boeing and Airbus decide whether to produce new aircraft. Profits (millions):

| BOEING / AIRBUS | Produce | Don't Produce |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| **Produce** | 10, -40 | 250, 0 |
| **Don't Produce** | 0, 200 | 0, 0 |

**Analysis:**

For **Boeing**:
- If Airbus produces: Boeing's payoffs are 10 (produce) vs. 0 (don't produce). Boeing prefers produce.
- If Airbus doesn't produce: Boeing's payoffs are 250 (produce) vs. 0 (don't produce). Boeing prefers produce.
- **Boeing's dominant strategy: Produce**

For **Airbus**:
- If Boeing produces: Airbus's payoffs are -40 (produce) vs. 0 (don't produce). Airbus prefers don't produce.
- If Boeing doesn't produce: Airbus's payoffs are 200 (produce) vs. 0 (don't produce). Airbus prefers produce.
- **Airbus does NOT have a dominant strategy**

**Nash Equilibrium:** Boeing produces; Airbus best-responds by not producing. Payoffs: (250, 0).

**Note:** Another equilibrium exists if one firm moves first (sequential game). The first mover (if it commits) deters entry.

## SECTION 7: GAME THEORY APPLICATIONS - REPEATED GAMES AND COOPERATION

### 7.1 Repeated Prisoner's Dilemma

In one-shot PD, both players confess (Nash equilibrium), leading to suboptimal (-5, -5).

**In infinitely repeated games or games with uncertain terminal date:**

**Tit-for-Tat Strategy:** Cooperate initially; in each subsequent round, do what opponent did last round.

- Against another tit-for-tat player: Both cooperate every round, earning (-2, -2) each round
- Against a defector: After defector breaks cooperation, punish by defecting the next round

**Conditions for cooperation in repeated games:**
1. High discount rate (firms care about future payoffs)
2. Credible threat of punishment
3. Ability to observe opponent's actions

**Business Application - Implicit Collusion:** Competing firms may cooperate (keep prices high) in repeated interactions if:
- Demand is stable and predictable
- Firms can observe prices
- Defection would trigger price war (harsh punishment)

In pharmaceutical industry, firms may coordinate pricing through industry norms (detente pricing) rather than explicit cartels.

### 7.2 Entry Deterrence

**Entry Game (Sequential):**
1. Incumbent sets price/capacity
2. Potential entrant observes and decides whether to enter
3. If entry occurs, both compete in Cournot game

**Incumbent strategies:**
- **Limit Pricing:** Set price low enough to make entry unprofitable, even though incumbent could charge higher price as monopoly
- **Credible Overinvestment:** Build excess capacity to signal aggressive response to entry; credibly commits incumbent to large post-entry output

**Application to Cortex:** Cortex might deter competitive entry by:
- Building network effects (switching costs increase as more hospitals use Cortex)
- Continuous innovation that stays ahead of potential entrants
- Exclusive partnerships with major hospital networks

## SECTION 8: PRICING STRATEGIES WITH MARKET POWER

### 8.1 First-Degree Price Discrimination (Perfect)

**Definition:** Charge each customer their maximum willingness to pay (WTP).

**Effect:**
- Producer captures all consumer surplus
- Firm profit maximizes: P = demand price at each unit
- Output same as perfect competition (P = MC for marginal unit)
- Total welfare same as competition, but distribution shifts to producer

**Reality:** Impossible to implement perfectly (firm doesn't know individual WTPs), but some firms approximate via:
- Auction formats (extract WTP through bidding)
- Personalized pricing (dynamic pricing based on customer data)
- Haggling/negotiation

**Challenge:** Legal/ethical concerns about price discrimination disclosure.

### 8.2 Second-Degree Price Discrimination (Quantity Discounts, Versioning)

**Definition:** Offer different prices for different quantities; firm doesn't explicitly condition on buyer type.

**Mechanism:** Through quantity brackets or product versions:
- Bulk discounts (buy more, lower per-unit price)
- Premium vs. basic product tiers
- Quality ladders

**Economics:** Customers self-select based on WTP. High-WTP customers buy premium; low-WTP buy basic.

**Example:** Cortex versioning:
- Basic tier ($5K/month): Core diagnostic features, limited API calls
- Premium tier ($15K/month): Full feature set, unlimited APIs, dedicated support
- Enterprise tier (negotiated): Custom configurations, on-premises deployment

**Cortex Application:** Second-degree discrimination captures higher WTP from large/wealthy hospital systems while serving smaller rural hospitals at lower tier.

### 8.3 Third-Degree Price Discrimination (Market Segmentation)

**Definition:** Divide market into segments; charge different prices to different segments based on elasticity.

**Rule:** Set higher prices in less elastic segments.

**Derivation:** Profit-maximizing condition is MR₁ = MR₂ = MC (equalize marginal revenues across segments).

Given MR = P(1 - 1/|PED|), a segment with lower elasticity (|PED| smaller) has higher MR, which means:
- Lower P needed to achieve MR₁
- Wait, that's backwards. Let me recalculate...

Actually: MR = P(1 - 1/|PED|), so:
- If |PED| larger (elastic): MR smaller for given P
- If |PED| smaller (inelastic): MR larger for given P

To set MR₁ = MR₂ with MR = P(1 - 1/|PED|):
- P₁(1 - 1/|PED₁|) = P₂(1 - 1/|PED₂|)
- If segment 1 more inelastic (|PED₁| < |PED₂|), then (1 - 1/|PED₁|) > (1 - 1/|PED₂|)
- Therefore P₁ < P₂

**So: Charge higher price to inelastic segment, lower price to elastic segment.**

**Application to Cortex in Brazil:**

Market 1: Large private hospital chains (São Paulo, Rio) - Inelastic demand
- Value cost-reduction; have capital; regulators encourage innovation
- Cortex can charge premium (e.g., $20K/month)

Market 2: Public sector hospitals (SUS) - More elastic demand
- Budget-constrained; price-sensitive
- Cortex charges lower price or subsidized tier (e.g., $5K/month)

Market 3: Rural/independent hospitals - Most elastic
- Least willing/able to pay
- Cortex offers basic tier or freemium model

## SECTION 9: TWO-PART TARIFFS AND BUNDLING

### 9.1 Two-Part Tariffs

**Structure:** Entry fee (fixed) + per-unit price (variable)
- Examples: Gym membership (fixed) + per-class (variable); Software license (fixed fee) + per-user (variable)

**Cortex Application:**
- Fixed component: Implementation and training ($100K initial)
- Variable component: $1K per month + $0.50 per patient encounter

**Economics:** Entry fee allows firm to extract consumer surplus; per-unit price manages quantity to profit-maximize.

**Optimization:** If all consumers identical, set per-unit price = MC (maximize quantity efficiency), capture all surplus via entry fee. If consumers heterogeneous in value, trade-off between:
- Higher per-unit price: Increases revenue per user but reduces volume
- Lower per-unit price: Increases volume but leaves consumer surplus uncaptured

### 9.2 Bundling (Pure and Mixed)

**Pure Bundling:** Sell products only as a bundle; cannot buy separately.
- Example: Microsoft Office (Word, Excel, PowerPoint sold as bundle)

**Mixed Bundling:** Sell products individually AND as a bundle (with discount).
- Example: Fast food "combo meal" (burger, fries, drink as bundle) vs. individual items

**Economics of Bundling:**

Pure bundling exploits correlation of valuations. Suppose:
- Consumer A values Product 1 at $100, Product 2 at $20
- Consumer B values Product 1 at $30, Product 2 at $90

Separate pricing (max prices to extract all surplus):
- P₁ = $30 (limited by B); sells 2 units, revenue = $60
- P₂ = $20 (limited by A); sells 2 units, revenue = $40
- Total = $100

Bundle pricing:
- Bundle = $110 (A's max = $120; B's max = $120)
- Sells 2 bundles, revenue = $220

Bundling is profitable because it mitigates heterogeneity in preferences.

**Cortex Application:** Bundle clinical decision support + outcome tracking + provider credentialing into single offering. Hospitals value different components differently, but bundling captures more total surplus than selling separately.

### 9.3 Tying (Leveraging Market Power)

**Definition:** Require purchase of product B to buy product A.
- Example: Microsoft bundling Internet Explorer with Windows

**Antitrust concern:** Tying can leverage monopoly power in market A to monopolize market B.

**Legitimate purpose:** Complement products (tying ensures compatibility); capture more consumer surplus (similar to bundling).

**For Cortex:** Avoid explicit tying (legal risk). Instead, ensure ecosystem compatibility and offer optional bundling (mixed bundling) so hospitals can adopt best-of-breed components if they prefer.

## SECTION 10: AUCTIONS AND MARKET DESIGN

### 10.1 Auction Formats

**English (Ascending) Auction:** Price rises; bidders drop out; last bidder wins at second-highest price.
- Common value item: Bidders estimate item's value differently (winner's curse risk)
- Private value item: Each bidder has independent valuation; winner pays near their own bid

**Dutch (Descending) Auction:** Price falls; first bidder to accept wins at announced price.
- Equivalent in revenue to sealed-bid first-price auction (revenue equivalence theorem)

**Sealed-Bid First-Price:** Each bidder submits sealed bid; highest bidder wins and pays their own bid.
- Bidders shade their bids (bid below true valuation) to reduce payment
- Strategic uncertainty: What will others bid?

**Sealed-Bid Second-Price (Vickrey Auction):** Highest bidder wins but pays second-highest bid.
- Dominant strategy: Bid true valuation (no incentive to shade bids)
- Revenue equivalence: Average revenue equals first-price auction under certain conditions

### 10.2 Nico's Problem Set Examples: Auction Theory

#### M6 Question 1: Sealed-Bid Second-Price Auction

**Scenario:** Maya and Alex bid for painting in sealed-bid second-price auction. Alex plans to buy and resell to other participants (private-value scenario). All other bidders plan to keep (personal use). Alex bid $10k and won.

**Question:** Can Alex resell at profit?

**Answer (Correct):** No, Alex will NOT be able to resell at a profit.

**Explanation:** In second-price sealed-bid auction, it's dominant strategy for bidders to bid true valuation (for private-value items). If all other bidders bid their true valuations for personal use, their valuations are private values (what they'd pay to keep forever). Alex wins because he bid $10k, meaning he bid more than the second-highest bidder's true personal valuation.

Since Alex paid the second-highest price, he paid more than what any other participant values the painting for personal use. Alex cannot sell it to any of them for a price higher than $10k (they won't pay more than their personal valuation). Thus, Alex cannot resell profitably.

**Key Insight:** Winner's curse in private-value auctions with resale opportunity. The winner "overpaid" relative to others' valuations.

#### M6 Question 2: First-Price Sealed-Bid Auction - Bid Shading

**Scenario:** Same painting, first-price sealed-bid auction. Maya willing to pay up to $10k.

**Question:** How much should Maya bid?

**Answer (Correct):** Less than $10k (exact amount depends on rivals' strategies, but definitely shade bid below true valuation)

**Explanation:** First-price auction is different from second-price. Maya must pay her own bid if she wins. If she bids $10k, she wins but pays $10k (consumes all her consumer surplus). She's better off bidding less (e.g., $8k) and still winning if rivals bid less. The optimal bid depends on beliefs about rivals' bidding strategies.

**Equilibrium result:** In symmetric equilibrium where all bidders have same valuation distribution, bidders shade bids such that expected payment is between first-price and second-price auction revenues.

#### M6 Question 3: Private-Value Auction - Bidder Valuations

**Scenario:** Elvis collection auction. Four bidders with private valuations:
- Aaron: $7,600
- Byron: $8,600 (highest)
- Caron: $8,300
- Daron: $8,000

**Question:** Outcomes in sealed-bid first-price vs. second-price auctions?

**Second-Price Auction:**
- Dominant strategy: Bid true valuation
- Winner: Byron ($8,600 bid), pays second-highest ($8,300)

**First-Price Auction:**
- All bidders shade bids; exact bids depend on equilibrium
- Winner: Likely Byron (highest valuation), pays less than $8,600 (shaded bid)

**Both auctions:** Byron wins but pays less in first-price (because he shades his bid below $8,600) compared to second-price (pays $8,300).

**Application:** Cortex procurement. If Cortex is selling access to hospital networks to pharma companies via auction, choose second-price to extract true valuations and avoid bidder manipulation.

#### M6 Question 4 & 5: Revenue Equivalence and Winner's Curse

**Key Principle:** Under certain conditions, first-price and second-price auctions generate equal expected revenue (revenue equivalence theorem).

**Winner's Curse (Common-Value Auctions):** When the item has same value to all, but each bidder estimates this value with error:
- Winner is likely bidder with highest (overestimated) value estimate
- Winner "overpaid" and faces negative surprise when true value realized

Example: Oil field auction. Competing oil companies estimate field's value. Winner likely overestimated value and faces losses.

**Cortex Application:** If Cortex acquires a data analytics startup via auction, Cortex (the bidder most optimistic about synergies) is most likely to overpay. Conduct rigorous due diligence to validate assumptions.

#### M6 Question 10: Second-Price Auction Calculations

**Scenario:** Vintage Omega Seamaster watch auction. Four bidders:
- Bidder 1: $300
- Bidder 2: $500
- Bidder 3: $800
- Bidder 4: $1,200 (highest)

**Second-price auction outcome:**
- Winner: Bidder 4 (bids $1,200, highest)
- Price paid: $800 (second-highest bid)
- Winner's net payoff: $1,200 - $800 = $400

This illustrates second-price auction mechanics: Highest valuation wins, but pays second-highest valuation. Winner extracts surplus (difference between personal valuation and price paid).

## SECTION 11: INFORMATION ECONOMICS

### 11.1 Asymmetric Information and Market Failures

**Adverse Selection (Hidden Type):** One party has private information about their type, and selection is adverse (high-risk types more likely to participate).

**Classic Example - Akerlof's Lemons Market:** Used car market. Buyers know distribution of quality (lemons vs. peaches) but not individual car's quality. Sellers know their car's quality.

- Equilibrium: Only low-quality cars trade (high-quality owners withdraw)
- Reason: Average market price reflects average expected quality (lemons + peaches); but owners of peaches know their quality is high, so they won't sell at average price
- Result: Market failure, quantity lower than first-best

**Moral Hazard (Hidden Action):** One party can take unobserved actions affecting outcome.

**Insurance Example:** Once insured, customer has less incentive to prevent loss (because insurer bears cost).
- Insured person drives recklessly
- Insurer can't perfectly observe driving behavior
- Result: Adverse selection + moral hazard = higher insurance cost, lower insurance coverage

**Healthcare Application - Moral Hazard:** Insurance shields patients from price signals. Patients consume more healthcare than they would if paying directly (overutilization). Insurers respond with:
- Copayments/deductibles (patient bears some cost)
- Prior authorization (approval required before services)
- Network restrictions (limited provider choice)

**Brazilian Healthcare Implication:** SUS provides universal coverage (moral hazard risk). Cortex's decision-support system can reduce unnecessary procedures, aligning clinical choice with social cost (value-based care).

### 11.2 Signaling (Spence Model)

**Definition:** High-quality type takes costly action to reveal type and separate from low-quality.

**Education Signaling:** Assume two types of workers (high/low productivity). Education is costly but provides no productivity boost. Yet high-productivity workers invest in education; low-productivity workers don't.

Why? Because education is relatively less costly for high-productivity workers (opportunity cost lower). In equilibrium:
- Educated workers seen as high-productivity; earn high wage
- Uneducated seen as low-productivity; earn low wage
- High-productivity workers invest in education (separating equilibrium)

**Relevance:** Signal must be costly and more costly for low-quality type to be effective.

**Healthcare Application:** Hospital accreditation, board certification, and adoption of advanced CDSS systems signal quality to patients. These are costly investments justified by high-quality hospitals but prohibitive for low-quality. Cortex could enhance signaling value by emphasizing outcomes measurement.

### 11.3 Screening (Firm Response to Adverse Selection)

**Definition:** High-information party offers menu of contracts to induce self-selection.

**Insurance Screening Example:** Insurer offers:
- Plan A: High deductible, low premium (attracts low-risk)
- Plan B: Low deductible, high premium (attracts high-risk)

Self-selection reveals type without insurer direct observation.

**Application to Cortex:** Offer tiered pricing based on hospital size/type:
- Small hospital plan: Limited modules, $5K/month (screens out large-capacity demand)
- Enterprise plan: Full suite, $50K/month (separates high-value users)

## SECTION 12: EXTERNALITIES, PUBLIC GOODS, AND NETWORK EFFECTS

### 12.1 Externalities and Market Failure

**Positive Externality:** Action by one party benefits others who don't pay (vaccination reduces disease spread).

**Negative Externality:** Action by one party harms others who aren't compensated (pollution, congestion).

**Market Failure:** Market equilibrium quantity differs from socially optimal quantity due to externalities:
- Positive externality: Market underproduces (not enough vaccination)
- Negative externality: Market overproduces (too much pollution)

**Solutions:**
- **Pigouvian tax:** Tax negative externality to align private and social cost
- **Pigouvian subsidy:** Subsidize positive externality to align private and social benefit
- **Coase Theorem:** If property rights clear and transaction costs low, market participants negotiate efficient outcome regardless of initial assignment

**Healthcare Application:** Medical training creates positive externality (better public health). Market undersupplies training; government subsidizes medical education.

### 12.2 Public Goods

**Definition:** Non-rival (consumption by one doesn't reduce availability for others) and non-excludable (can't prevent others from consuming).

**Examples:** National defense, clean air, open-source software

**Market failure:** Free-rider problem. Since non-excludable, consumers don't pay; firms undersupply.

**Solution:** Government provision or regulation (e.g., broadcast licenses for radio, mandatory vaccination requirements).

### 12.3 Network Externalities (Positive Externalities in Technology)

**Definition:** Value of network increases as more users join.

**Examples:**
- Phone networks (more value as more people can be called)
- Social media (more value as more friends join)
- Software ecosystems (more value as more apps available)

**Market Dynamics:**
- Tipping point: Once network reaches critical mass, growth accelerates
- Winner-take-most: Network externalities favor dominant platform; competitive alternatives struggle

**Cortex Application:** CDSS has moderate network externalities. As more hospitals adopt Cortex, data pool increases, AI models improve, value increases for all users. This creates flywheel effect and justifies premium pricing to earlier adopters (they help build network).

## SECTION 13: HEALTHCARE MICROECONOMICS AND BRAZILIAN MARKET CONTEXT

### 13.1 Healthcare Demand Characteristics

**Price Elasticity of Demand - Highly Inelastic:**
- Emergency/acute care: |PED| ≈ 0.1-0.3 (very inelastic)
- Chronic care: |PED| ≈ 0.3-0.7 (inelastic)
- Elective/cosmetic: |PED| ≈ 1.0-2.0 (more elastic)

**Reasons for Inelasticity:**
- Urgency (no time to shop)
- Life-and-death stakes (price irrelevant relative to outcome)
- Insurance shields patients from price signals (moral hazard)
- Information asymmetry (patients can't evaluate quality-price tradeoff)

**Implication for Cortex:** Pricing for acute care applications (sepsis detection, MI diagnosis) can be premium (inelastic demand). Pricing for elective procedures (joint replacement scheduling) must be more competitive.

### 13.2 Moral Hazard in Health Insurance

**Brazilian Context:**
- Public system (SUS) provides universal coverage with no/minimal copayments → high moral hazard
- Private insurance (supplemental) covers 25-30% of population; typically includes copayments/restrictions
- Out-of-pocket (40%+ of healthcare spending in Brazil) creates price sensitivity

**Moral Hazard Manifestations:**
- Unnecessary emergency room visits (no cost to patient)
- Overutilization of diagnostic tests
- Defensive medicine (extra procedures to reduce liability risk)
- Length-of-stay inflation (insurers monitor but patients have limited incentive to refuse)

**Cortex Opportunity:** Decision-support systems that reduce unnecessary procedures generate value across all segments:
- For SUS: Reduce fiscal burden, improve efficiency
- For private insurers: Reduce claims costs, improve margins
- For patients (out-of-pocket): Reduce unnecessary out-of-pocket spending

### 13.3 Adverse Selection in Health Insurance

**Issue:** High-risk individuals more likely to purchase comprehensive insurance.

**Brazilian Context:** Regulatory minimum coverage requirements reduce adverse selection risk. But within supplemental insurance market, adverse selection exists:
- Elderly, chronically ill prefer comprehensive plans
- Young, healthy purchase basic plans
- If basic plan pricing doesn't reflect higher risk of adverse selection, insurers lose on basic tier

**Cortex Application:** Help insurers identify high-risk individuals early, enabling more precise pricing and targeting preventive interventions (reducing claims downstream).

### 13.4 Physician Agency and Supplier-Induced Demand

**Agency Problem:** Physicians are patients' agents (advise on medical care) but have own financial incentives (fee-for-service reimbursement incentivizes volume).

**Supplier-Induced Demand:** Physicians may induce demand beyond medically necessary level because they benefit from procedures.

**Evidence in Brazil:** Regional variation in cesarean rates (some hospitals >80% vs. clinical evidence of 20-30% optimal) suggests some induced demand.

**Cortex Value:** Clinical decision-support that provides evidence-based guidelines reduces variation and aligns incentives toward appropriate care, not maximum volume.

### 13.5 Hospital Market Power (Local Monopolies)

**Structural Characteristic:** Many Brazilian cities have single major hospital or small number of private hospitals with bargaining power over insurers.

**Market Structure:** Oligopsony (few buyers - insurers) negotiating with oligopoly (few sellers - hospitals). Outcome depends on bargaining power.

**Implication for Cortex:** Cortex can enhance hospital negotiating power by demonstrating superior outcomes/efficiency (improves insurer's cost-containment argument). Can also help insurers by providing utilization/quality benchmarks.

### 13.6 Drug Pricing Economics

**Global Context:** Pharmaceutical firms have patent-protected monopoly during patent life.

**Pricing Strategy:** Tiered by country/patient ability-to-pay:
- US market: Premium pricing ($10K+ per dose for biologics)
- Emerging markets (Brazil): 20-50% of US price
- LDCs: Further discounts

**Brazilian Regulation:** CMED (Câmara de Regulação do Mercado de Medicamentos) regulates drug prices; firms must justify pricing relative to production costs.

**Price Elasticity Paradox:** Drugs have inelastic demand (patients must take), yet price ceilings common (governments can't afford open-ended drug budgets).

3. **Regulatory Navigation:** Early engagement with ANVISA on classification. If designated as medical device, pursue registration immediately (don't wait for competition). If classified as software service, document clinical processes to defend against future reclassification.

## GLOSSARY OF KEY MICROECONOMIC TERMS

- **Adverse Selection:** Selection of high-risk types into insurance markets due to asymmetric information
- **Bundling:** Selling multiple products as a package (often at discount) rather than separately
- **Dominant Strategy:** Strategy that's best regardless of opponent's choice
- **Elasticity:** Responsiveness of quantity demanded to price changes (or other variables)
- **Equilibrium:** State where no player has incentive to unilaterally deviate
- **Externality:** Uncompensated impact on third party (positive or negative)
- **Marginal Cost:** Cost of producing one additional unit
- **Moral Hazard:** Taking excessive risk because insured (hidden action)
- **Nash Equilibrium:** Strategy profile where each player's strategy is best response to others
- **Network Externality:** Value of network increases as more users join
- **Price Discrimination:** Charging different prices to different customers for same product
- **Two-Part Tariff:** Fixed entry fee + variable per-unit fee pricing structure
- **Willingness-to-Pay (WTP):** Maximum price customer willing to pay for a good
